MODULE 1 Introduction to Information & Communication Technology

What is ICT? Definition ▼

ICT (Information and Communications Technology) is the infrastructure and components that enable modern computing.

It generally means all devices, networking components, applications, and systems that combined allow people and organizations (businesses, nonprofits, governments) to interact in the digital world.

Technology has always been a means of communication and it continues to reshape our world — encouraging new habits, new ways to work together, and new opportunities across every industry.

Uses of ICT in Our Daily Lives (4 Main Areas) Types ▼
  • Communication – Enables chatting, email, voice mail, and social networking. ICT allows communication even across great distances at the cheapest cost.
  • Job Opportunities – ICT enables organizations to operate more efficiently. Employers now expect basic ICT skills. Jobs can be searched online using smartphones, laptops, etc.
  • Education – Supports audio-visual packages, interactive exercises, remote learning, and flipped classrooms. Teachers who are digitally literate develop students' higher-order thinking skills.
  • Socializing – Social media changed how people find partners, access news, and organize for change. Platforms like Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, and LinkedIn help people share interests globally.
Positive Impacts of ICT on Society Key Concept ▼
  • Access to Information – Better and cheaper communications (VoIP, Instant Messaging); access to worldwide information.
  • Improved Education – Distance learning, online tutorials, interactive multimedia, virtual reality.
  • New Tools & Opportunities – Digital cameras, photo editing, screen readers for visually impaired people.
  • Communication Cost Savings – VoIP instead of phones, email instead of post, video conferencing instead of travel.
  • Information Management – Data mining for targeted advertising; improved stock control and cash flow.
  • Security – Encryption keeps data safe from unauthorized access.
  • Global Participation – ICT allows people to participate in a wider, worldwide society.
  • New Jobs Created – Systems analysts, programmers, software engineers, help desk operators, trainers.
Negative Impacts of ICT on Society Key Concept ▼
  • Job Loss – Automation replaces manual operations (e.g., robots on assembly lines). Job export to countries with lower operating costs. Fewer workers needed to do the same amount of work.
  • Reduced Personal Interaction – People may feel isolated and unhappy without face-to-face contact.
  • Reduced Physical Activity – Can lead to obesity, heart disease, and diabetes.
  • High Cost – Hardware and software are expensive to purchase and maintain; requires specialist staff.
  • Competition – Organizations may lose to others that offer the same service for less money on a national or international scale.

MODULE 2 History of Computer: Basic Computing Periods

Definition of Computer & 3 Principal Characteristics Definition ▼

A computer is a programmable machine — an electronic device that manipulates information/data with the ability to store, retrieve, and process data. It is any device that aids humans in performing computations or calculations.

Three Principal Characteristics:

  • Responds to a specific set of instructions in a well-defined manner.
  • Can execute a pre-recorded list of instructions.
  • Can quickly store and retrieve large amounts of data.
Note: The first use of the word "computer" was recorded in 1613, referring to a person who carried out calculations — not a machine.

Early Computing Devices (Timeline) History ▼
2400 B.C.
Abacus – Invented in Babylonia; used for basic arithmetic. Familiar Chinese form used around 500 B.C.
1614
Napier's Bones – Invented by John Napier; rods for multiplication, division, roots.
1622
Slide Rule – Invented by William Oughtred; based on Napier's logarithms; used for multiplication, division, trigonometry.
1642
Pascaline – Invented by Blaise Pascal; limited to addition and subtraction; too expensive.
1672
Stepped Reckoner – Invented by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz; could add, subtract, multiply, and divide automatically.
1820
Arithmometer – Invented by Thomas de Colmar; first reliable, commercially successful, mass-produced calculating machine.
1822 & 1834
Difference Engine & Analytical Engine – Charles Babbage; first mechanical computer; designed to tabulate polynomial functions.
1840
First Computer Programmer – Augusta Ada Byron suggested binary system to Babbage and wrote programs for the Analytical Engine.
1843
Scheutzian Calculation Engine – Per Georg Scheutz; based on Babbage's engine; the first printing calculator.
1881
Jacquard Loom – Joseph-Marie Jacquard; mechanical loom controlled by punched cards.
1890
Tabulating Machine – Herman Hollerith; used to summarize information and accounting.
1936–1938
Z1 – Konrad Zuse; first programmable computer; used punch tape for input/output.
1939–1942
Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC) – John Atanasoff & Clifford Berry at Iowa State University; first electronic digital computing device.
1943
Harvard Mark 1 (IBM ASCC) – Howard H. Aiken; first electro-mechanical computer; 8ft high, 50ft long, 5 tons.
1946
ENIAC – John Presper Eckert & John Mauchly; first electronic general-purpose computer; 680 sq ft, 30 tons; used vacuum tubes.
1949
First Computer Company – Electronic Controls Company, founded by Eckert and Mauchly.
1981
First Portable Computer – Osborne 1, released by the Osborne Computer Corporation.

MODULE 3 The Web and the Internet

The World Wide Web – Overview & Inventor Definition ▼

The World Wide Web (WWW) consists of information organized into Web pages containing text and graphic images. It is a larger collection of interconnected documents/content that uses hypertext links (highlighted keywords and images) to lead to related information.

  • Web Site – a collection of linked Web pages with a common theme or focus.
  • Home Page – the main page that all pages on a Web site link back to.
Inventor: Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee (TimBL) — English engineer and computer scientist. Professorial Fellow at the University of Oxford and professor at MIT.

Key Features of Web 2.0 Types ▼
  • Folksonomy – Allows users to categorize/arrange information using freely chosen keywords (e.g., tagging).
  • Rich User Interface – Content is dynamic and responsive to user input (e.g., site that shows local content).
  • User Participation – Not just the website owner puts content; others add through comments, reviews, and evaluation.
  • Long Tail – Services offered on demand rather than one-time purchase. Like subscribing to a data plan charged by usage or bandwidth.

Web 2.0 Examples:

  • Social Networking – Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Google+, Pinterest, Tumblr, Instagram
  • Blogs – WordPress, Blogger, Tumblr (posts in reverse chronological order)
  • Wikis – Wikipedia, Wikibooks, Wikiversity, Wikidata, Wikinews, Wikispecies, MediaWiki, Wikiquote

The Internet – Definition, History & Fathers History ▼

The Internet ("net" = network of networks) is the largest computer network in the world that connects billions of users. The word comes from interconnection + network. Generally, nobody owns the internet.

Jan 2, 1969
ARPA (Advanced Research Project Agency) started an experimental computer network. No central server — every computer had equal importance. If one or two nodes were destroyed, the network still worked.
1982
The word "Internet" officially started being used.
1986
First "Free Net" created at Case Western Reserve University.
1991
US government allowed business agencies to connect to the internet.
Father of the Internet: Vinton Gray Cerf — American Internet pioneer; co-developer of TCP/IP with Bob Kahn.
